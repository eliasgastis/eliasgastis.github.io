🔬 research.dat _□✕

Most of the galaxies in the background of this webpage don't look too much like our own Milky Way. They are most often found in galaxy groups and clusters, and unlike the Milky Way, they lack spiral arms and don't actively form stars. Despite their reputation of being completely devoid of molecular gas, the fuel for star formation, recent discoveries have revealed that these galaxies still contain trace amounts that co-evolve with their host galaxies across cosmic time.

During my time at RSAA, I worked as part of the MAGPI research group to investigate the molecular gas content of these elliptical 'quiescent galaxies' (QGs). Probing the interstellar-medium of these galaxies helps us understand when and how their star-formation ceased, and remains an open question today. My research combined data from the world's most powerful radio interferometer (ALMA), and draws on integral-field data collected by the MUSE instrument on the world's most advanced optical observatory (VLT), to provide original constraints of the molecular gas content of QGs at z ~ 0.3.

▶ The Milo Mission Academy

In 2024 I took part in the Milo Mission Academy, a 12-week space-workforce training program run by Arizona State University's Milo Space Science Institute in partnership with AROSE (Australian Remote Operations for Space and Earth). The program offered students from across Oceania the chance to work in multidisciplinary teams, and to design a lunar mission end-to-end, following the formal design reviews used in the NASA space mission life-cycle. Along the way we heard from experts across a breadth of discplines in NASA mission planning, and learnt about the key challenges and considerations present in a mission to one of the most extreme environments in our solar system, the permanently shadowed regions at the lunar south pole.

The culmination of the project was a 250 page Preliminary Design Review (PDR), and a chance for us to pitch our project to shareholders. You can access our PDR above to learn more about our proposal!

▶ The SPIDER mission

Our team's submission was the Shadowed Polar Ice Discovery and Exploration Rover (SPIDER). SPIDER's objective is to understand how water-ice and other volatile resources are distributed on the Lunar south pole. Such materials are central to the success of NASA's Artemis program, as in-situ resource utilisation is a key prerequisite for establishing a sustained human presence on the Moon in the next decade. Additionally, SPIDER can help us understand how the water-ice was deposited on the Lunar surface, helping us understand the history and formation of our solar system.

In place of conventional wheels, SPIDER uses a four-legged hopping locomotion system, drawing on recent advances in robotics. This approach evades risks posed to traditional wheeled-rovers, and helps us negotiate the tricky and unstable terrain present on the Lunar surface.

As Lead Scientist, I oversaw the mission's science narrative. This involved tracing the overarching science goals down to their physical observables. A sound understanding of the scinece informed the selection of the instruments in the science payload, and the performance requirements they were designed to meet. The hardest part? Organising all of this within a set of size and cost constraints!
